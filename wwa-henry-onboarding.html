The Session Timer
90 minutes. That's the window. The timer keeps us honest.
▼

Every session runs on a 90-minute countdown. The timer starts once mobility is complete — not when we walk in. That means arriving on time isn't just courteous, it's critical. Every minute lost before the timer starts is a minute taken from the session and the shoot.

Why it matters: 1 hour of precision beats 2 hours of wandering. The timer enforces the standard. When it's running, we move with purpose.

For you: this means your training stays tight and intentional. No dead time between sets. No losing track of the clock.

Diary Frames
The recurring content captured every session. Two angles per exercise.
▼

Diary Frames are short cinematic clips captured on the first working set of each exercise. No talking, no commentary — just a fly-on-the-wall look at what training actually looks like. Think of them as silent, high quality windows into the session. Music gets added in post.

Each diary frame is built from two locked-off angles shot back to back, then edited together into one seamless clip. Here's exactly how it flows:

01
Angle 1 — Mount and capture 2–3 reps. Lock the camera off, hit record, I perform. Don't follow the movement.
02
Angle 2 — Reposition and capture 2–3 reps from the second angle. Different perspective on the same movement.
03
Back to Angle 1 — 2–3 more reps.
04
Back to Angle 2 — 2–3 reps to failure. This is the closing shot.

In the edit, these four clips cut together into one fluid sequence — A1, A2, A1, A2. The result is a cinematic multi-angle look at a complete set, from mounting to failure. The goal isn't to show the set exactly as it happened in real time — it's to present the full set from multiple angles so the viewer experiences it as one complete performance. Even if the angles were captured across multiple mini-sets, in the edit it reads as one.

First working set only Anamorphic mode 4:5 frame Locked off — no handheld No audio

For you: the Session Master has 3 pre-populated diary frame slots per session with a checklist for each. Work through them in order and check each angle off as you capture it. Low barrier — mount, record, move on.

When a Curated Shoot is on the schedule, we know exactly what we're shooting, where, and why before we walk in. There's no guessing on the day.

🔐
Vault Preparation
The content bank we're building toward. Two weeks of posts at all times.
▼

The Vault Preparation page tracks where we are against our content target. The goal is simple: always have two weeks of content ready to post before anything goes live. That means edits complete, captions ready, nothing rushed.

As we shoot and edit, the vault fills up. When it hits the two-week target, we post. The vault keeps us ahead of the algorithm instead of scrambling to feed it.

Critically — we track every piece by which content pillar it falls under. We cannot afford to edit only one type of content and end up with a vault full of diary frames and nothing else. The vault needs to be balanced across pillars: Contrast, Origin, Mindset, Education, Client, Proof Physique. If we're missing a pillar, that becomes the priority in the next session.

→
Diary frames from each session go into the vault as carousel content
→
Pump checks go in as Proof Physique posts
→
Curated shoots go in as Reels once edited — filed under their pillar
→
Every piece logged with its pillar so we always know what we have and what we're missing

Your role: file footage same day or next morning after each session. The vault only works if content moves through quickly after capture.

Editing Expectations
✂️
Edit Within the Week. No Exceptions.
Everything shot needs a target and a completion date.
▼

Everything we capture in a session needs to be edited within one week of the shoot date. That's the rule. Not a guideline — a rule. And the purpose of that rule is simple: to keep your life moving.

In the past, we've sat on footage for weeks. The vault builds up, the backlog compounds, and suddenly we're spending sessions in the gym that produce nothing because editing hasn't kept up with shooting. That ends here. The output of the content needs to match the output of the training session. We shoot it, we edit it, we post it. That's the cycle.

Diary frames and Henry training clips are straightforward assemblies — two angles, cut together, grade applied, done. These should be the quickest wins in your edit queue. Start there.

→
Start editing the day after the session. While the footage is fresh and the context is clear.
→
Diary frames and training clips first. These are the quickest wins — get them done and into the vault.
→
Curated shoots have priority in the edit queue if they were captured that session.
→
Every piece of footage gets a completion date before the next session happens.

Why this matters: content sitting in a folder unedited for weeks doesn't just delay posting — it compounds Henry's workload and stalls the vault. Staying one week behind on edits is manageable. Two weeks starts to hurt. Three weeks breaks the system.

That's the whole system. Session Master runs the session. Notion tracks the content. The vault builds the bank. Every session we run this and the machine stays fed. Questions — bring them to the session and we'll sort it out in person.
