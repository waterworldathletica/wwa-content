Full Script ▼
It was late. I'd just walked out of one of the worst arguments of my life with my girl.

No destination. No music. Just the city.

I ended up at Fort York — a park in the middle of Toronto. Summer days, picnics, music with friends.

But it was dark that night. Empty.

The deeper I walked in, the darker it got. Through skeletal trees and eroded ground from years of the city living on top of it.

On the other side of the park — a faint light. And the distant sound of a long horn.

It didn't feel how you'd imagine it would feel to walk toward a train.

I was just walking toward the only light I could see.

With every step, that haunting idea slowly became more real. Like I was testing how real this thought actually was.

I was getting closer.

On the edge of the park, there's a perimeter fence. I climbed it. Walked across the gravel and finally met the set of rails that run through Toronto.

On my left — a faint set of headlights and a distant horn. Growing brighter and louder.

And I just stood there. Weighing everything out.

I wasn't crying. I wasn't panicking. I was genuinely assessing.

Every person that stole from me. Every promise that dissolved. Every moment of worthlessness. Every failure.

One last test.

I took my phone out. And I called my brother.

I spoke to him like I had nothing left to lose.

I listened to him as he tried to soften my heart.

Those headlights are closer now. And this thing is roaring.

Something inside pulled me to take a step backwards.

Thank god I did.

Not because I believed in myself. Not even because I believed him. But for his sake.

That step is the reason any of this exists.

The training. The coaching. Every conversation. Every person I've worked with since.

All of it was birthed on the other side of that step.

This is where Waterworld began.
